# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Technical writers and content publishers — people who write software documentation, technical guides, or long-form creative content and believe that **a clear and legible reading environment is priority one**, without sacrificing beauty and grace. They care about their readers. They see documentation as a craft, not a chore. They will install this theme specifically because of how it makes reading feel — and how that reflects on their work.

Secondary: Developers who maintain OSS or product documentation and want their docs to signal the same quality as their code.

## Product Purpose

An Astro Starlight theme where readability and beauty are the same discipline.

Exquisitus is built on a simple conviction: that excellent documentation requires both rigorous legibility and aesthetic ambition — and that these are not competing goals. It proves the case by example. Every page is a demonstration that craft and clarity are the same thing at different scales.

Success looks like: a reader who doesn't notice the design because they're too busy reading, but who would feel its absence immediately.

## Brand Personality

Confident, masterful, useful.

The voice of a senior colleague who explains something well — no hedging, no decoration, but clearly someone who cares. Think: the pleasure of reading MDN documentation on a page that looks as considered as the New York Times.

## References

- **New York Times Online** — typographic mastery, editorial authority, beautiful content-first presentation; trust is built before the reader reads a word
- **Readability Guidelines wiki** — bold single-minded focus on legibility, effective simplicity that never feels boring, willingness to use larger typefaces
- **MDN Web Docs** — beauty through utility; care as craft; fantastic navigability that proves documentation can be a labor of love
- **Eleventy Excellent** — dared to use larger accessible fonts without sacrificing color or layout; demonstrates that ambition and practicality coexist

## Anti-references

- Hacker / terminal aesthetic (monospace everything, green-on-black, cyberpunk)
- Generic SaaS docs — Mintlify / Docusaurus defaults: clean white, blue primary, forgettable sidebar, no visual ambition
- Cream / sand / warm-neutral body backgrounds as a proxy for "warmth" — warmth belongs in the typography and brand color, not the surface
- Side-stripe accent borders, gradient text, glassmorphism decoration
- AI-scaffold patterns: eyebrows above every section, identical card grids, hero-metric templates

## Design Principles

1. **Content is sovereign** — every design decision is in service of the reader. If an element doesn't serve comprehension or orientation, remove it.

2. **Legibility IS beauty** — the two are not in tension; this theme proves it. Contrast, spacing, and type scale are aesthetic tools, not accessibility checklists.

3. **Generous text** — default to larger, not smaller. The Readability Guidelines and Eleventy Excellent both dared to use bigger type. So do we. Readers deserve more space.

4. **Care made visible** — the subtle things signal craft: consistent rhythm, precise spacing, type that breathes, colors that don't fight the content. These are noticed subconsciously and felt immediately.

5. **Accessible by conviction** — WCAG compliance is the floor, not the ceiling. Inclusivity strengthens the aesthetic; it never weakens it. Reduced motion, readable type, high contrast — first-class.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

- WCAG 2.2 AA minimum across all surfaces
- Extra inclusivity where it meets the vision: reduced motion support, care for color-blind and low-vision readers, semantic HTML
- Larger default type scale (principle #3) is itself an accessibility choice
- Color contrast treated as a design constraint, not a compliance afterthought
